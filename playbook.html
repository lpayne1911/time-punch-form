1. Core Philosophy

The goal is not to trade everything. The goal is to find a small group of strong stocks, wait for repeatable setups, enter only when the setup confirms, and cut losses before one bad trade destroys your account.

Trade leaders. Wait for pullbacks or bases. Enter on confirmation. Cut losses quickly. Repeat.

Most new traders struggle because they jump between styles, chase indicators, lack a repeatable system, and become emotionally attached to losing trades.

2. Trading Style

Recommended: Swing Trading

For a beginner trying to make their first $10,000, swing trading — not day trading — is recommended. Swing trading lets you:

  • Hold trades for more than one day.
  • Capture larger moves.
  • Avoid needing extreme intraday precision.
  • Reduce emotional pressure from minute-by-minute candles.
  • Let strong stocks make bigger directional moves.

Day trading is more skill-intensive: it requires fast decisions, emotional toughness, and precision many beginners have not yet developed.

3. Starting Capital

Suggested starting range: $1,000 to $2,500. Don't use lack of capital as an excuse — stack enough to trade with a meaningful account size.

Practical rule

Do not trade with money you cannot afford to lose. Before trading live you should have:

  • Emergency savings separate from trading money.
  • A defined max loss per trade.
  • A written trading plan.
  • No need to withdraw trading profits immediately.

4. Equipment Needed

You do not need a multi-monitor trading desk. One computer is enough; two screens at most. Focus on becoming a trader, not looking like one.

Minimum setup: laptop or desktop, phone, a charting platform (e.g. TradingView), a broker account, a watchlist, and a trading journal.

5. Build Your Watchlist

Create a focused list of 20 stocks maximum. It should contain only market leaders — not random stocks, cheap stocks, or popular names that are underperforming.

6. Stock Selection Criteria

Criteria 1 — Must be a market leader

The stock should be outperforming the S&P 500 by at least 2x. If the S&P 500 is up 5% YTD, the stock should be up at least 10%. If the S&P is up 8%, the stock should be up at least 16%.

Criteria 2 — Leading industry

The stock should belong to a sector showing strength — find strength stacked on strength. Examples mentioned: semiconductors, memory, energy, oil & gas, equipment-related names.

Criteria 3 — Above key moving averages

Preferably above the 20-day SMA and 50-day SMA, ideally also above the 200-day. This confirms the stock is still in an uptrend.

7. What Not to Trade

Avoid stocks that are:

  • Underperforming the S&P 500.
  • Below major moving averages.
  • Falling year-to-date while the market is rising.
  • Popular but not currently leading.
  • Cheap only because they are weak.
  • Too volatile without clean structure.

Even a famous stock should be rejected if it is underperforming the S&P 500. The method prioritizes current strength, not popularity.

8. The Two Main Setups

Setup A — Pullback Buy

After a stock makes a strong move up, then pulls back into support:

  1. Stock is a market leader.
  2. Stock makes a strong move up.
  3. Stock pulls back.
  4. Pullback finds support.
  5. Price reclaims a key level.
  6. Volume confirms the move.
  7. Entry occurs after confirmation.

Triggers: price reclaims the previous all-time high, reclaims the 20-day moving average, or breaks back above a clearly marked resistance level.

Setup B — Base Breakout

After a strong upward move, the stock trades sideways in a tight range:

  1. Strong move up.
  2. Price consolidates sideways.
  3. Candles become tight.
  4. Stock holds support.
  5. Price stays above the 20-day moving average.
  6. A clear pivot level forms.
  7. Price breaks above the pivot.
  8. Volume confirms breakout.

Basing is a period where price tightens rather than moving wildly — this can signal accumulation before the next move.

9. Key Chart Terms

Parabolic move: a sharp, powerful move upward. Don't chase after it has already happened — wait for a pullback or base.

Pullback: a controlled drop after a strong move — profit-taking, not necessarily weakness.

Base: a tight sideways consolidation after a strong move. A good base has tight candles, clear support, a clear pivot, reduced chaos, and price holding above important moving averages.

Pivot: the breakout level — usually the top of the base or the candle high price needs to break.

Reclaim: when price falls below a key level, then moves back above it. Reclaims of the 20-day MA or previous all-time high can be entries.

10. Entry Rules

Do not enter just because a stock is strong. Enter only when the chart gives confirmation:

  • Entry 1 — Previous high reclaim: price pulls back, then reclaims a previous all-time high or major resistance.
  • Entry 2 — 20-day MA reclaim: price pulls back near/below the 20-day MA, then closes back above it.
  • Entry 3 — Base breakout: price forms a tight base and breaks above the pivot.
  • Entry 4 — Pullback breakout: price breaks out, retests the breakout area, then continues upward.

11. Volume Confirmation

Watch volume on breakout or reclaim moves. A better setup has:

  • Above-average volume.
  • Volume expansion on the breakout.
  • A strong daily candle closing near the high.
  • Buyers stepping in at support.

If price breaks out on weak volume, the move may be less reliable.

12. Risk Management Rules

Losses are unavoidable, but large losses are often caused by ego and emotional attachment.

Caution Zone — Down 10%

Warning territory. Ask: Is the setup still valid? Did price lose support? Did volume confirm selling? Did the stock fall below the 20- or 50-day MA? Did the broader market turn?

Review Zone — Down 15%

Reassess seriously. Ask: What went wrong? Was the entry too late? Did I chase? Did I ignore the stop? Has the chart broken structure?

Exit Zone — Down 20–25%

The trade is gone. Preserving 70–80% of your capital gives you the ability to take another trade instead of sitting in a collapsing position.

Tighter beginner rule

For a newer trader, make it stricter: Warning −5% · Serious review −8% · Exit −10% to −15%. Cutting losses at 10–15% is the cleaner beginner rule.

13. Position Sizing

Risk only 1% to 2% of account value per trade.

Example with a $2,500 account: 1% risk = $25 max loss; 2% risk = $50 max loss. If your stop is 10% below entry, then with $25 risk your max position is $250, and with $50 risk it's $500. This protects you from one bad trade destroying the account.

14. Trade Management

Before entering — write down

Ticker · Industry · S&P 500 performance · Stock YTD performance · Setup type · Entry price · Stop price · Target area · Reason for trade · Invalidation level.

While in the trade — monitor

  • Does price hold the breakout level?
  • Is volume supporting the move?
  • Is price staying above the 20-day MA?
  • Is the stock still outperforming?
  • Has the broader market changed?

Exit conditions

Exit if the stop is hit, price breaks support, price loses the 20-day MA and fails to reclaim, the breakout fails, the thesis is invalidated, or the loss reaches your max allowed percentage.

15. Profit-Taking Rules

Sell in pieces

  • 25% to 50% after a strong move.
  • More if price becomes extremely extended.
  • Remaining shares if the stock loses the 20-day MA or breaks trend.

Don't get greedy. The goal is not to catch the entire move — it's to repeatedly capture clean portions of strong moves.

16. Weekly Routine

Weekend — build/update watchlist

  • S&P 500 year-to-date performance.
  • Which stocks are up 2x or more vs. the S&P.
  • Which sectors are leading.
  • Which stocks are above the 20- and 50-day SMA.
  • Which charts are forming pullbacks or bases.

Daily — review your 20-stock list

Look for pullbacks into support, reclaims of key levels, breakouts above pivots, volume expansion, and failed setups to avoid.

After every trade — journal

Entry · Exit · Setup · Mistake (if any) · Whether rules were followed · What the loss or win taught you. Losses contain data — study them to improve.

18. Simple Operating System

  1. Find leaders — 20 stocks up at least 2x the S&P 500.
  2. Filter by industry — prioritize leading industries.
  3. Confirm trend — price above the 20- and 50-day MAs.
  4. Wait — don't chase parabolic moves; wait for pullback, base, reclaim, or breakout.
  5. Enter on confirmation — only when price breaks or reclaims a key level.
  6. Cut losses — around 10–15% as a beginner; don't let a small loss become a disaster.
  7. Repeat — same watchlist, same setup, same rules, same journal, same discipline.

19. The Main Lesson

You do not need to be the smartest trader. You need a repeatable behavior-recognition system: find leaders → wait for pullback/base → enter confirmation → cut losses → repeat.

The danger is not losing — losses are normal. The danger is refusing to cut losses, chasing moves, jumping between strategies, and trading without a repeatable process.
